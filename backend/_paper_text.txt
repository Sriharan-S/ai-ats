Abstract. This paper presents the design of a sophisticated machine learning pipeline for an "AI-Powered Student Profile Tracker." The system utilizes an enhanced feature set de- rived from student-provided data, applying advanced feature engineering to create predictive interaction variables. Data is dynamically sourced from public APIs like GitHub, LeetCode, and Codeforces, in addition to resume parsing. The methodology involves robust data preprocessing, including time-series analysis of student commits and problem-solving velocity, TF-IDF Vectorization for resume keywords, and Standard Scaling for numerical stats. To address the "black box" problem in AI-driven hiring, an Explainable AI (XAI) strategy is employed. A suite of optimized ensemble models, including XGBoost and Random Forest, are combined into a Voting Classifier to generate a "Job Match Score." The final model’s predictions are interpreted using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), providing transparent feedback to the student. This output directly powers a skill-gap analysis engine, which generates personalized learning path recommendations, thus creating a closed-loop system for continuous student improvement.
INTRODUCTION
The transition from academia to the professional world presents a significant and persistent challenge for students in engineering and technology disciplines. In these fields, practical skills and verifiable project experience are often valued as highly as formal academic qualifications. However, the primary instrument for assessment, the traditional resume, is a static, self-reported document. This method of evaluation is inherently subjective, time-consuming for reviewers, and, most critically, often fails to capture the full spectrum of a student’s practical abilities, such as coding habits, problem- solving consistency, and collaborative development experience.
Furthermore, the proliferation of automated Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) in the hiring pipeline has created a new obstacle. Many of these systems are primitive "black boxes," rejecting a high volume of candidates based on simple keyword-matching failures without providing any actionable feedback [4]. Students are often left guessing why they were rejected, receiving only vague advice like "needs more projects" or "lacks relevant skills." This lack of transparency results in an inefficient and demoralizing feedback loop, where students are unable to target their efforts to fill specific, in- demand skill gaps.
This paper details the architecture and development (Phase I) of an "AI-Powered Student Profile Tracker." The primary objective is to construct a highly accurate, reliable, and most importantly, *interpretable* predictive model. This system is designed to generalize across diverse student profiles by leveraging complex, time-series data from disparate, verifiable sources. We aim to overcome the limitations of the static resume by creating a holistic and dynamic profile for each student.
Our system is engineered to collect and store historical, time-stamped data from public developer platforms. These include GitHub (to analyze commit frequency, languages used, and repository contributions), LeetCode (to assess problems solved, difficulty levels, and ranking progression), and Codeforces (to track competitive programming ratings and contest history). This longitudinal data is then combined with traditional resume parsing to create a rich feature set. We engineer sophisticated interaction and ratio features, such as "Commit Consistency Score," "Problem-Solving Velocity" (problems solved per week), and "Project-Skill-Keyword-Overlap." These features are designed to uncover hidden patterns that are far more predictive of a student’s true technical ability and dedication than a simple list of projects on a resume.
The core of our methodology utilizes an advanced ensemble learning strategy coupled with an Explainable AI (XAI) framework. A diverse set of powerful base classifiers, including Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) [2] and Random Forest [3], are trained and then combined into a more stable and accurate Voting Classifier. This model predicts a "Job Match Score" by comparing the student’s dynamic profile against a specific job description.
To address the critical issue of model opaqueness a major barrier to user trust the pipeline integrates an XAI framework using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) [1]. For every score generated, the system provides transparent, feature-level justifications. This allows students to understand *why* they received their score and which specific skills or activities (e.g., "high commit frequency," "lack of SQL projects") had the most impact. This predictive output is then channeled directly into a Skill Gap Analysis and Personalized Learning Path Recommender, which guides the student with specific, actionable advice. This creates a closed-loop system for continuous improvement, directly contributing to the UN Sustainable Development Goal 4 (Quality Education) by utilizing AI to enhance student employability and technical proficiency.
RELATED WORK
The literature surrounding student assessment and automated hiring systems highlights a complex interplay of technical, academic, and practical factors. This review synthesizes findings related to dynamic profiling, automated hiring biases, and the application of ensemble learning in predictive models. 
Dynamic vs. Static Profiling
The inadequacy of the traditional resume as a sole predictor of technical ability is a well-documented problem. Hendler et al. [9] emphasize the importance of "holistic and dynamic developer profiles." Their research suggests that models based on longitudinal data, such as tracking a student’s progress and activity over time, significantly outperform models trained only on static, resume-based data. They demonstrate that dynamic activity metrics hold a much higher predictive power for technical roles. This strongly supports our system’s core design choice to integrate APIs from platforms like GitHub [6], LeetCode, and Codeforces. These platforms provide verifiable, time-series data on a student’s actual coding habits and problem-solving skills, moving beyond self-reported claims.
Bias and Transparency in Automated Hiring
The proliferation of automated hiring tools has been a subject of significant research, particularly concerning their potential for bias and lack of transparency. Gregory et al. [10] analyze the risks and benefits of automated hiring, stressing that data-driven prediction can minimize human bias but can also introduce new, systemic biases if not carefully designed. The "black box" nature of many commercial ATS tools, as highlighted by Raghavan et al. [4], often leads to inconsistent outcomes. Furthermore, Boerma et al. [11] conducted a global study revealing that high ATS rejection rates are often driven by trivial factors like document formatting and keyword- matching failures, rather than a genuine lack of qualification. This body of work establishes a clear need for systems that are not only accurate but also transparent and fair. Our use of XAI is a direct response to this problem, aiming to provide equitable and understandable assessments. 
Explainable AI (XAI) in Guidance and HR
The "black box" problem is not unique to hiring. Ryding [12] investigates the non-technical motivations behind career choices and underscores the potential of XAI systems to provide transparent reasoning for skill-gap analysis. Such transparency, which we aim to provide via SHAP [1], can measurably improve student motivation and facilitate shared decision-making between a student and their career mentors. By explaining *why* a skill gap exists, the student is empowered to act on the recommendation, rather than simply being told what to do.
Ensemble Learning in Predictive Classification
From a technical standpoint, the use of machine learning for skill classification and prediction is well-established. Deivapraba et al. [13] developed ML-based diagnostic models, showcasing the potential of ensemble learning for career- related predictions. Their work demonstrates the versatility of algorithms like Random Forest [3], XGBoost [2], and ensemble voting techniques, which are directly applicable to our student skill prediction framework. Similarly, Kothai et al. [14] show how combining multiple classifiers and integrating interpretability mechanisms improves both predictive accuracy and student trust in educational technology. Our system builds on these findings by fusing a robust ensemble (a Voting Classifier) with a state-of-the-art XAI framework (SHAP).
Finally, related research into Personalized Learning Environments (PLEs) [8] has shown the transformative potential of integrating AI with data from various educational APIs. These systems provide continuous monitoring and timely recommendations necessary to improve student outcomes, especially in self-directed learning environments. Our project sits at the intersection of these fields, acting as both an assessment tool and a personalized learning recommender.  
SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE AND METHODOLOGY
The proposed system is architected as a modular, end-to-end data pipeline designed to ingest, process, analyze, and provide feedback on a student’s professional profile. The architecture is divided into five primary modules, which are executed sequentially to create a closed-loop feedback system for the user. The overall data flow is illustrated in Fig. 1. 
Data Collection Module
The foundation of the system is a robust data aggregation module that moves beyond static resumes. Data is collected from two primary sources:
User-Provided Data: Students provide two key inputs: their resume (in .pdf or .docx format) and the text of a target job description. The resume is parsed using NLP libraries to extract unstructured text, which is then segmented into categories such as education, experience, projects, and declared skills.
Dynamic API Data: The system queries public APIs from key developer platforms to gather real-time, longitudinal data. This is crucial for validating resume claims and assessing practical, consistent effort. This includes:
GitHub API [6]: We access user events and repository endpoints to retrieve commit timestamps, repository languages, and contribution statistics.
LeetCode API: We query a public API to track problem-solving statistics, including the number of problems solved by difficulty (easy, medium, hard) and the user’s contest ranking and submission history.
Codeforces API: We retrieve user ratings and contest history to provide a measure of competitive programming performance.
This multi-source approach creates a rich, time-series feature set that provides verifiable evidence of a student’s practical skills and consistency, rather than relying on self-reported information alone.
Data Preprocessing Module
Raw data from the collection module is heterogeneous, consisting of unstructured text, categorical data (like programming languages), and numerical statistics. This data must be transformed into a structured, numerical format suitable for machine learning models. This is handled by a ‘scikit-learn‘ ‘ColumnTransformer‘ pipeline with two main branches:
Text Preprocessing (NLP): Text from the student’s resume and the target job description undergoes a standard NLP pipeline. This includes conversion to lowercase, removal of punctuation, tokenization, and the removal of common English stop-words. The cleaned tokens are then vectorized using a TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) vectorizer [5]. This method is superior to simple word counts as it identifies and assigns higher weights to keywords that are highly relevant to a specific document (e.g., "TensorFlow" in a machine learning job description) but rare across the general corpus of all documents.
Numerical Preprocessing: All statistical data retrieved from the APIs (e.g., commit_count, problems_solved, codeforces_rating) is scaled using a ‘StandardScaler‘. This transformation remaps each feature to have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one. This normalization is essential to prevent features with large absolute values (like commit_count) from disproportionately influencing the model’s predictions compared to features with smaller ranges (like codeforces_rating).

Advanced Feature Engineering
This is one of the most critical stages of the pipeline, where we move from raw data to high-signal predictive features. We create a new set of interaction and ratio-based features, including:
Commit Consistency Score: A feature calculated from the timestamps of GitHub commits. A student who commits code consistently (e.g., 5-10 commits every week) will score higher than a student who uploads 10 projects in a single day, as it demonstrates consistent effort.
Problem-Solving Velocity: This feature measures the number of LeetCode problems solved over a rolling 30-day window, providing insight into a student’s recent and active preparation.
Skill Keyword Density: The raw count of keywords from the job description that are also found in the student’s resume, normalized by the length of the resume.
API Skill Validation Ratio: A feature that measures the overlap between skills listed on the resume (e.g., "Python," "Java") and the languages detected in the user’s public GitHub repositories. A high ratio indicates that the student’s self-reported skills are backed by practical project work.
Project-Skill Overlap: A TF-IDF-based cosine similarity score between the text of the student’s "Projects" section on their resume and the text of the "Responsibilities" section of the job description.
AI-Powered ATS (Ensemble Model)
The core predictive engine is an AI-Powered Applicant Tracking System (ATS). Instead of relying on a single model, which may be prone to a specific type of error, we employ an ensemble learning strategy to improve robustness and generalizability [7].
Base Estimators: We utilize two powerful and dissimilar models as base estimators to capture different patterns in the data:
XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) [2]: A highly efficient and optimized implementation of the gradient boosting framework. It is chosen for its renowned speed, scalability, and exceptional ability to handle the sparse data generated by the TF-IDF vectorizer while also modeling complex non-linear relationships in the numerical features.
Random Forest [3]: An ensemble method based on bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating). It operates by constructing a multitude of deep decision trees at training time on different random subsets of the data and features. This approach makes it highly robust to overfitting and noise, providing a stable and independent "second opinion" to the boosting model.
Voting Classifier: The trained base estimators are combined into a ‘VotingClassifier‘ with ‘voting=’soft’’. This meta-estimator does not just take a majority vote; it averages the predicted probabilities from both the XG- Boost and Random Forest models. This averaging process typically produces a more nuanced, better calibrated, and reliable final "Job Match Score" that leverages the confidence of each base model, resulting in superior performance compared to any single classifier alone.
Explainable AI (XAI) Module
A key innovation of this system is its inherent transparency. A simple "Job Match Score" of 65% is meaningless to a student without an explanation. To avoid the "black box" problem, we integrate a robust Explainable AI (XAI) module using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) [1]. SHAP is a game theory-based approach that computes the contribution of each feature to the final prediction, ensuring that the influence of each feature is fairly distributed. 
For every "Job Match Score" generated, the XAI module provides a human-readable justification. It programmatically identifies which features (e.g., presence of keyword "Python", total_commits > 200, high LeetCode ranking) contributed positively to the score and which features (e.g., absence of keyword "SQL", low_commit_frequency) contributed negatively. This transparency is crucial for building student trust. For instance, the system can generate a "force plot" showing that "Skill: SQL" pushed the score up, while "Low Commit Consistency" pushed the score down. This output directly powers the recommendation engine.
Recommendation Engine
The final module closes the feedback loop by translating the predictive and analytical outputs from the ATS and XAI modules into tangible, personalized, and actionable advice. This module functions as a skill-gap analysis and recommender system [8].
Gap Identification: The engine programmatically parses the SHAP values to identify the features that had the largest negative impact on the student’s score. These are flagged as the most critical skill gaps.
Personalized Roadmap Generation: It then maps these identified gaps (e.g., "SQL", "Data Structures", "Web Dev Project") to a pre-defined content library of learning resources, online courses, and sample project ideas.
Feedback Loop: The system presents the student with their score, the SHAP explanation, and a prioritized, personalized set of recommendations. For example: "Your profile is a 65% match. To improve for this ’Backend Engineer’ role, focus on 1. SQL (This was a key missing skill), 2. Building a full-stack project (Your project experience was weighted low)." This transforms the application from a passive tracker into an active, intelligent career coach.
IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
The system is developed as a web application to ensure accessibility for students and career counselors. The technology stack was chosen for its robust ML support, rapid development capabilities, and scalability. 
Technology Stack
Backend Framework: The server-side logic, API endpoints, and model-serving are handled by Flask, a lightweight Python web framework. Its simplicity is ideal for an ML-driven application.
Machine Learning: The core ML pipeline is built using Scikit-learn for preprocessing, ensemble methods, and evaluation. The XGBoost library is used for the gradient boosting model, and the SHAP library is used for model interpretability.
Data Handling: Pandas and NumPy are used for all data manipulation, feature engineering, and in-memory data structuring.
Frontend: The user interface is a single-page application (SPA) built with modern JavaScript (ES6+), HTML5, and CSS3. It communicates with the Flask backend via asynchronous Fetch API calls.
Database: While Phase I focuses on in-memory processing, the full-scale application is designed to use PostgreSQL to store user profiles, historical API data, and job description metadata. This allows for long-term tracking and model retraining.
API Integration
Interfacing with external APIs requires handling authentication, rate limiting, and data parsing. The GitHub API, for instance, is accessed using a Personal Access Token (PAT) to ensure a higher rate limit. All API calls are wrapped in ‘try-except‘ blocks with exponential backoff and retry logic to handle network failures or rate-limit exceptions gracefully. Data received from these APIs, typically in JSON format, is parsed and flattened into a Pandas DataFrame before being fed into the preprocessing pipeline.
EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
As this paper details the architecture of a Phase-I project, a formal "Results" section is not yet available. Instead, we propose a robust experimental framework for Phase II to validate the system and quantify its performance.
Dataset Curation and Labeling 
A high-quality dataset is the prerequisite for a meaningful model. We will construct this dataset as follows:
Profile Collection: We will solicit resumes from 500+ senior-year students and recent graduates from our institution. For each student, we will run our data collection module to gather corresponding GitHub, LeetCode, and Codeforces data.
Job Description Collection: We will scrape and collect 200 real-world job descriptions for entry-level tech roles (e.g., "Software Engineer," "Data Analyst," "Frontend Developer") from various job portals.
Ground Truth Labeling: This is the most critical step. We will create 10,000 student-job pairs. Each pair will be independently evaluated by a panel of three domain experts (two technical recruiters from partner companies and one senior professor). They will label each pair on a scale of 1 (Poor Fit) to 5 (Excellent Fit). The average score will be normalized and used as the ground truth "Job Match Score." For classification, scores ≥ 3.5 will be labeled as "Match" (1) and others as "No Match" (0).
Baseline Model for Comparison 
To prove the value of our dynamic profiling and ensemble model, we will compare its performance against a strong baseline. The baseline will be a traditional, static ATS model based on TF-IDF and Cosine Similarity. This model will only use the text from the student’s resume and the job description, calculating their match score based purely on keyword overlap, mimicking how many current ATS systems operate.
Evaluation Metrics 
The system’s performance will be evaluated using 5-fold stratified cross-validation. Given the likely imbalanced nature of the dataset (more "No Match" than "Match" instances), we will focus on the following metrics:
Accuracy: The overall proportion of correct classifications.
Precision (for the ’Match’ class): The ability of the model to avoid false positives. This is critical for recruiters, who do not want to review unqualified candidates. 

Recall (for the ’Match’ class): The ability of the model to find all true positives. This is critical for students, who do not want to be missed for a job they are qualified for. 

F1-Score (weighted): The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, providing a single, balanced metric for im- balanced datasets.
Mean Absolute Error (MAE): For the regression task of predicting the 1-5 score, MAE will measure the average error of our "Job Match Score."
EXPECTED RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Anticipated Model Performance 
We expect the Ensemble Voting Classifier to significantly outperform the Cosine Similarity Baseline. The baseline model’s reliance on keywords alone is brittle; it can be "gamed" by students stuffing keywords into their resumes and cannot differentiate between a student who *claims* to know Python and one who *actively* codes in Python daily.
Our model, enriched with engineered features like Commit_Consistency_Score and Problem-Solving_Velocity, will be able to capture this crucial difference. We anticipate our model will achieve a weighted F1-Score at least 20-25 points higher than the baseline.
Expected XAI Insights 
The SHAP analysis will be used to validate our feature engineering hypotheses. We expect to see:
High positive SHAP values for features like	Commit_Consistency_Score, Problem Solving_Velocity and API_Skill_Validation_Ratio, confirming that consistent, practical effort is a key predictor of a good "match."
High negative SHAP values for a *mismatch* between resume keywords and API-verified languages.
High importance for specific TF-IDF keywords, which will vary by job description, confirming the model is correctly identifying role-specific requirements.
This will allow us to quantitatively prove that *how* a student works (consistency, activity) is as important as *what* they list on their resume.
Ethical Considerations and Limitations 
A critical part of our discussion will focus on ethical implications. Our system relies on publicly available data from APIs. This raises privacy concerns. To mitigate this, the system is designed as an "opt-in" tool for students, who must authenticate and provide consent for the system to access their data.
Another limitation is the "cold start" problem. Students with no public GitHub or LeetCode profiles will have many missing values. The model must be trained to handle this gracefully, likely relying more heavily on their resume text, but it must not unduly penalize them. The XAI module will be crucial here, as it can explicitly tell a student: "Your score is based only on your resume. To improve its accuracy, consider making your developer profiles public." 
CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This paper has presented the complete architecture of an "AI-Powered Student Profile Tracker," a system designed to provide dynamic, interpretable, and actionable feedback to students. By aggregating data from static resumes and real- time developer APIs, our system moves beyond traditional, subjective assessment methods. The core of the system is a robust ensemble learning model (Voting Classifier) that generates a "Job Match Score," which is then explained by a SHAP-based XAI module. This explainability is the key that unlocks the system’s true value: it powers a skill-gap analysis and recommendation engine that provides students with personalized learning roadmaps.
Upon successful validation, future work will focus on three key areas. First, we will expand the data collection module to include more platforms, such as Kaggle (for data science projects), HackerRank, and Stack Overflow (to gauge com- munity engagement). Second, we plan to enhance the recommendation engine by integrating a Large Language Model (LLM) to generate more dynamic and conversational learning roadmaps, rather than relying solely on a static content library. Third, the system will be deployed as a pilot program within our institution’s career services department, allowing for a longitudinal study.
We will track student improvement over time after receiving feedback from the system, measuring the real-world impact of our tool on student employability and skill acquisition. This will allow us to continuously retrain and improve the model based on real hiring outcomes.
